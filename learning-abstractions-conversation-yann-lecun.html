Learning Abstractions: A Conversation with Yann LeCun

Yann LeCun (NYU & Meta FAIR) & James M. Manyika (Google Alphabet) — Dædalus, American Academy of Arts & Sciences

Computer Science Artificial Intelligence World Models Open Science 2026 Interview
Source
Dædalus, Winter/Spring 2026
TL;DR

In a wide-ranging conversation for Dædalus, Yann LeCun argues that current LLMs cannot reach human-level intelligence because they lack world models, cannot plan, and predict tokens instead of learning abstractions. He proposes Joint-Embedding Predictive Architectures (JEPA) as the path forward — systems that learn to predict at the right level of abstraction, the way scientists do, rather than reconstructing every pixel or token. He also makes a forceful case that open science is not optional for AI progress — secretive labs cannot solve problems that require entire research communities.

Limitations of Current AI Systems

LeCun identifies three fundamental constraints that prevent LLMs from achieving human-level intelligence:

1. The Prediction Problem

Physical systems contain noise and stochastic elements that make pixel- or token-level prediction impossible. Scientists have always addressed this through abstraction — eliminating unpredictable details and making predictions in simplified representation spaces. LLMs, by contrast, are forced to predict every next token, which is fundamentally at odds with how understanding works.

2. No World Models

LLMs lack causal models that integrate state-action-next state sequences. Without these, they cannot run virtual experiments, optimize plans, or reason about the consequences of actions. This is the gap between pattern matching and genuine understanding.

3. Training Inefficiency

Post-training through supervised learning (RLHF, expert annotation) becomes prohibitively expensive at scale. You cannot annotate examples for every possible scenario a system might encounter. A human child learns to navigate the physical world from a few years of experience; LLMs require orders of magnitude more data and still fail at basic physical reasoning.

JEPA & Learning Abstractions

Joint-Embedding Predictive Architectures offer LeCun’s proposed alternative to generative models. Rather than reconstructing pixel-level details (as diffusion models or autoregressive models do), JEPA learns representations that eliminate unpredictable information while retaining what is predictively useful.

The core insight mirrors scientific methodology: the choice of abstraction level determines the discipline. Predicting atomic interactions gives you quantum chemistry; predicting molecular behavior gives you biochemistry; predicting cellular behavior gives you biology. Each level deliberately discards detail that is irrelevant at that scale.

JEPA applies this principle to machine learning: instead of predicting in pixel space (where much is unpredictable), predict in a learned embedding space where the unpredictable parts have been factored out. Longer-horizon predictions naturally require progressively higher abstraction levels, accepting information loss for tractability.

AMI vs. AGI: Why Terminology Matters

LeCun rejects the term “artificial general intelligence” on both practical and theoretical grounds. Human intelligence itself is not general — we are spectacularly narrow in most domains and only excel at a few. The term “AGI” implies a threshold that doesn’t exist and feeds narratives about superintelligent machines that “only happens in bad sci-fi movies.”

He advocates instead for Advanced Machine Intelligence (AMI) — systems that exceed human capability in specific domains while remaining tools under human direction. The rebranding is not cosmetic: it shifts the conversation from existential risk fantasies to engineering roadmaps about what capabilities we can actually build and how to deploy them responsibly.

Search-Based Reasoning

Current LLMs generate outputs autoregressively — each token is produced in a single forward pass with no opportunity for optimization or backtracking. LeCun argues this is fundamentally insufficient for reasoning.

Effective AI requires two components working together: a generator that produces candidate solutions and an evaluator that scores them. Chess demonstrated this decades ago through tree search — the engine doesn’t just produce one move, it explores and evaluates thousands of possibilities.

The challenge for AI is performing this search in continuous representation spaces rather than discrete token sequences. This is where world models become essential: they provide the simulator in which candidate action sequences can be evaluated before being executed in the real world.

AI for Scientific Discovery

LeCun envisions AI as research assistants working under scientist direction rather than autonomous investigators. The relationship mirrors a PhD advisor: scientists provide objectives and constraints; AI systems explore the space and report findings.

Promising Domains

  • Materials science & protein design — exploring vast combinatorial spaces that humans cannot search manually
  • Chronic disease & cancer treatment — modeling complex biological pathways from organelles to organ systems
  • Energy storage & catalysis — optimizing chemical processes through simulation
  • Cosmological simulations — validating theories about dark matter through phenomenological modeling

Curiosity as a Learning Mechanism

LeCun frames curiosity as uncertainty-driven learning. When a model’s predictions fail, it adjusts its parameters. When predictions feel uncertain, the agent experiments — like infants systematically dropping objects to test gravity. This mirrors the scientific method: hypothesis, intervention, observation, model correction.

Reductionism vs. Phenomenology

Complex emergent properties — superconductivity at magic angles, intelligence emerging from neurons — resist reduction to fundamental equations. Data-driven phenomenological approaches, bridging microscopic and macroscopic scales, offer practical alternatives where pure theory cannot reach. Three fundamental questions remain: what comprises the universe, what is life, and how does the brain function.

The Case for Open Science

LeCun makes one of the conversation’s strongest arguments on open-source AI, going beyond ideology to strategic necessity:

  • Peer scrutiny prevents self-deception. Without publication and community review, researchers cannot distinguish genuine progress from artifacts. Science requires adversarial verification.
  • Talent follows openness. Scientists want to publish. Secretive companies struggle to recruit and retain top researchers because the work cannot be shared.
  • Open tools accelerate everyone. TensorFlow, PyTorch, and JAX transformed AI research by democratizing access to infrastructure. The competitive advantage comes from engineering specifics, not theoretical breakthroughs.
  • Hard problems require communities. No small team — however talented — can solve problems at the frontier of AI alone. The claim that a secretive group will develop transformative AI “only happens in bad sci-fi movies.”

On geopolitical concerns about sharing AI research: LeCun contends that the benefits of open collaboration far outweigh the risks. Restricting research doesn’t prevent adversaries from developing capability — it only ensures your own community falls behind.

Conclusion

LeCun’s vision is coherent and contrarian: the current LLM paradigm is a dead end for achieving human-level intelligence, not because of scale limitations but because of architectural ones. Token prediction cannot learn world models; without world models there is no planning or reasoning; without planning or reasoning there is no intelligence. The path forward runs through JEPA, learned abstractions, search-based reasoning, and open scientific collaboration — not through bigger language models.

So What?

Whether or not you agree with LeCun’s specific prescriptions, his diagnosis points to a real tension in AI: the industry is scaling a paradigm (next-token prediction) that even its most prominent practitioners acknowledge cannot reach its stated goals. For anyone building on LLMs today, the practical implication is clear — treat them as powerful pattern matchers, not reasoning engines, and watch the JEPA/world-model research line for the next architectural shift.
