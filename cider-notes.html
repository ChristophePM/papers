Research Notes

CIDER 2026

Causal Inference and Digital Experimentation Roundtable — invite-only discussions on experimentation, causal inference, and AI evaluation hosted by Netflix.

Chatham House Rules Spotify attendees: Mårten Schultzberg, Kyle Kretschman Authors: Mårten Schultzberg
Microsoft / GitHub Meta Anthropic Amazon Roblox Spotify Airbnb OpenAI Netflix UC Berkeley Columbia Cornell

Key Takeaways

01 — Throughput

Experiment velocity is the bottleneck

Better short-term proxies, smarter stopping rules, and measurement quality are more pressing than ever as AI accelerates idea generation faster than testing can keep up.

02 — Metadata

Compound learnings across experiments

Companies want portfolio-level learning but lack high-quality metadata. AI agents could auto-enrich experiments with missing context like bet ownership and DoD mapping.

03 — Eval

Evaluating AI agents is genuinely hard

Both Anthropic and OpenAI flagged ever-growing eval sets, flakiness, and dynamic user interactions. Evan Miller published a paper on applying basic stats to eval uncertainty.

04 — Tooling

Agents need deterministic tools

Give agents narrow statistical packages instead of open-ended tasks. Netflix open-sourced an OCI agent for this. Aligns with Spotify's experimentation platform approach.

05 — Autonomy

Autonomous experimentation within a year

Broad optimism that agents will set up, launch, monitor, and analyze experiments autonomously. Everyone agrees agents will at least handle planning, setup, and summarization.

06 — Policy

Inference as a utility optimization problem

Frame alpha and power choices as business policy — weighing false positive/negative costs, harmful-variant exposure, and sample size. Ongoing PZN work supports this.

1

Moving from Outcome to Mechanism

Core tension: launching on noisy evidence vs. genuinely learning something. When teams ship without understanding why a treatment worked, they risk chasing noise and building on a weak foundation.

Practical challenge: distinguishing whether a treatment did what was intended from a systems perspective, or whether users simply responded positively to whatever the treatment produced. These are distinct questions that get conflated.

On formal models: The consensus leaned toward encouraging structural causal thinking rather than mandating DAGs universally. The ROI of formalization scales with product maturity — when core metrics move easily, intuition is enough; at the margin, rigorous causal specification pays off.

AI opportunity: Enthusiasm for using AI to extract implicit causal hypotheses from code, strategy docs, and test specs rather than requiring experimenters to write them down.

Broader conclusion: Data scientists may need to become stronger theorists. Structural thinking helps teams reason about Goodhart's law and unanticipated effects, even without full formal models.

2

Hybrid Evaluation Ecosystems

Three approaches discussed:

  • Pre-filter: Use LLMs to screen out bad ideas before online experimentation
  • Augment: Combine LLM synthetic users with historical data as priors to reduce online sample size
  • Replace: Full silicon testing with synthetic users (surprising optimism, but long-term outcomes remain a challenge)

What works now: LLMs show genuine promise in constrained applications — analyzing session recordings, generating personas for survey-like questions, sessionizing activity logs, and helping economists build structural models quickly. Consistent advice: give agents narrow, well-defined jobs.

Key limitation: LLMs are better at interpolating than extrapolating. Simulating known behaviors is tractable; predicting responses to genuinely novel launches is much harder. Cold start is particularly resistant.

Near-term consensus: A tiered system where simulation and proxy metrics filter out bad ideas before online testing — not replacing A/B testing altogether. Online experiments remain essential for guardrail functions and tuning with real traffic.

3

The Shift from Analyst to System Architect

Role bifurcation: A large population of generalist product builders using AI for analysis, surrounded by fewer deep experts building evaluation systems and guardrails. Implies fewer specialist data scientists overall.

Durable core skills: Problem framing (translating ambiguous business problems into well-formed questions) and answer validation (checking an output actually addresses the question) were repeatedly cited as the enduring human contribution.

Soft skills debate: Some argued communication remains essential for influencing decision-makers. Others countered that if agents write for agents, traditional persuasion may erode. A sharp point: much of "soft skills" is navigating principal-agent problems — if AI gains full org context, those frictions may dissolve.

Junior pipeline problem: Junior hiring has already contracted at several companies, with some teams replacing junior DS with AI tools. The skills that make senior people effective were developed through exactly the low-level work AI is now automating. How the next generation develops judgment is an open question.

For academia: Focus on critical thinking and skepticism — the ability to identify what's wrong with an analysis quickly. Deep implementation knowledge matters less; interrogating and validating outputs matters more.

4

Evaluating AI Products & Non-Determinism

Framing: Fundamentally a measurement problem, not an experimental design problem. A/B testing and always-valid inference remain sound; what's hard is defining success for products with enormous user journey spaces.

Not entirely new: Non-determinism and high-dimensional product spaces existed in search and recommendation. But the pace and dimensionality have increased to a point where existing tooling can't keep up without major investment.

Specific challenges:

  • High-dimensional outcome spaces where metric count scales with product complexity
  • Heterogeneous user interactions with no clean counterfactual
  • Continuously changing models making results non-generalizable across time
  • Users simultaneously updating their own expectations and skills

Quality guardrails: Because AI failure modes are categorically different from bad recommendations, continuous monitoring beyond A/B test windows is essential. LLM-as-judge scales qualitative evaluation, but keeping human ratings fresh is an open problem.

Call to action: Academics urged practitioners to publish more on real-world AI measurement challenges. Academic work is almost entirely focused on benchmarks and is far behind what industry is grappling with. More industry-academia collaboration on causal evaluation of deployed AI systems was described as a major research opportunity.

Open Challenges (Roundtable)

  • Velocity vs. validity: AI accelerates idea generation but experiments remain slow — pressure to ship faster risks "throwing things at the wall"
  • Metric sensitivity: North star metrics lose sensitivity as products mature, making meaningful changes harder to detect
  • Long-term measurement: Can't run holdouts for months; teams launch without metadata to evaluate decisions retrospectively
  • Independence assumptions: Classical A/B testing breaks down with model spillover and marketplace interference effects
  • Portfolio learning: How to learn across thousands of experiments and use accumulated knowledge to guide what to test next
  • Regression detection: Shipping a worse model without catching it is a near-nightmare scenario; choosing the right eval subset is its own hard problem
  • Agent autonomy: No clear framework for how much autonomy to grant agents in interpreting results and making recommendations
